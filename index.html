What is Mosi-oa-Tunya?

Mosi-oa-Tunya means "the smoke that thunders" in the local Tonga language of Zimbabwe.

Who named it Victoria-Falls and why?

David Livingstone named it in honour of his monarch, Queen Victoria of England in 1855.

Interesting facts about Victoria-Falls/Mosi-oa-Tunya

It is the world's largest waterfall.
It is 915m above sea level.
It is 1708m wide, with an average depth of 100m.
Water sprays from the Falls can be seen 30km from Lusaka and 50km from Zimbabwe.
It is a breeding site for endangered species such the Taita Falcon andthe Black Eagle.
There is ancient lava flows, ancient stone artefacts and ancient hunting tools.
It is a UNESCO heritage site.

Pre-Colonial History

Archeological findings aroung the Falls have revealed
Homo habilis artefacts which date back 3 million years ago.
Middle age stone tools of about 50 000 years ago have been
found and Later stone Age hunting weapons and tools from about
2000 years ago have been found around the Falls. The Khoisan tribe
of the Stone Age era where the first inhabitants of the area.
They were later displaced by the Tonga people, who still live
the area today.

Colonial History of the Falls

Eouropeans started to settle in Victoria-Falls/Mosi-oa-Tunya around
1900 at the behest of Cecil John Rhodes's expansionist desires.
Rhodes facilitated for a bridge to be built across the Zambezi river.
During the British colonial rule, the Falls became a popular tourist
attraction.

Best times to visit

Between February to May, soon after the summer rains.
January is best month for white-water rafting.
